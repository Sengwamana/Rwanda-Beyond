Traditional farming practices in Rwanda, particularly in maize production, still depend heavily on manual monitoring of soil moisture, environmental conditions, and pest incidence. This limits the farmer’s capacity to respond proactively to irrigation needs or emerging threats such as Fall Armyworm infestations. As a result, inefficiencies in water use, delayed pest intervention, nutrient imbalance, and avoidable yield losses continue to affect productivity.Advances in the Internet of Things (IoT), wireless sensing, and artificial intelligence (AI) provide powerful opportunities to address these limitations. IoT sensor networks allow continuous real-time monitoring of soil and environmental parameters, while AI-driven analytics enable predictive modelling for irrigation scheduling, pest detection, and fertilization guidance. When combined with low-cost communication technologies such as GSM, SMS, and USSD, these innovations become accessible to smallholder farmers in rural areas even those without smartphones or internet access.This study proposes the design and development of an IoT-based Smart Maize Farming system enhanced with AI decision support. The system integrates GSM-enabled soil moisture, soil temperature, air humidity, and soil nutrient sensors to monitor field conditions and uses AI models for irrigation optimization, FAW early detection, and fertilizer recommendations. Focusing on maize farmers in Rwamagana District, the research aims to demonstrate how affordable digital technologies can improve farm management, reduce risks, and increase productivity through data-driven decision-making.1.2 Background of the StudyThe global agricultural sector faces mounting pressures such as a rising population requiring more food, decreasing natural resources, climate variability, and increasing threats from pests and diseases. Traditional farming techniques  relying on fixed planting calendars, periodic manual irrigation or rainfall, and extensive application of inputs are increasingly inadequate to sustainably meet growing demand and environmental constraints. In response, the concept of “smart farming” (sometimes called Agriculture 4.0) has gained traction integrating modern information and communication technologies (ICT), sensor networks, automation, data analytics, and artificial intelligence (AI) to support precision agriculture, resource optimization, and realtime monitoring (Li, et al., 2020; Karar, Alotaibi, Al Rasheed & Reyad, 2021).For example, IoTbased systems have been demonstrated to monitor environmental parameters (soil moisture, temperature, humidity), automate irrigation, and reduce unnecessary water use leading to both water savings and improved yield outcomes. (Karar et al., 2021) Such technologies promise to transform farming from a reactive practice to a datadriven, adaptive, and resilient enterprise globally, especially under increasing climate uncertainty.Despite this promise, global adoption is uneven. Advanced smart farming is more common in developed countries or large commercial farms where infrastructure (electricity, stable internet, capital) is available. For smallholder farmers who form the backbone of agriculture in many developing regions barriers such as cost, lack of infrastructure, technical expertise, and scalability often prevent adoption. Thus, while smart agriculture has shown its potential to modernize and sustainably intensify food production globally, its benefits remain concentrated, and there is a global need to design inclusive, lowcost, contextappropriate smartfarming solutions for smallholders.In Africa, agriculture remains critical for livelihoods, food security, and economic development. A significant share of the population depends on smallholder farming; yet agricultural productivity often remains low, partly due to outdated practices, climate vulnerability, poor input management, and lack of timely information. (Ayim, Kassahun, Tekinerdogan & Addison, 2022). Recognizing this, stakeholders across the continent have promoted ICT-based agricultural innovations, including mobile-based advisory services, marketinformation systems, weather alerts, and basic digital platforms for farm management. Indeed, many smallholders now receive market, weather, and input-related information via SMS or voice; and mobile phones have become a central medium for connecting farmers to knowledge and services. (Ayim et al., 2022, Abdulai, Quarshie & Duncan, 2023)However  as shown in a recent study among smallholder farmers in Northern Ghana while digital agriculture tools have expanded, engagement remains limited for many farmers. The study found most rural farmers rely only on basic mobile phones and rarely access internet-based or complex digital services, digital literacy is low; and sustained use of digital tools often fails after initial exposure. (Abdulai, Quarshie & Duncan, 2023) A systematic review of ICT adoption in African agriculture (covering 2010–2019) further highlights that the majority of adopted technologies are text or voice-based, radio, or partial mobile apps  with very limited use of advanced IoT, sensor-based or automated agriculture solutions. (Ayim et al., 2022) The authors argue that infrastructural gaps, limited policy frameworks, and lack of technical capacity constrain broader adoption of “smartfarming” technologies across smallholder farmers.In more recent years, digital agriculture is gaining renewed interest on the continent. As of 2023, digital agricultural tools (across advisory, input supply, market linkage, etc.) have proliferated, and a growing number of smallholders have been registered in digitalservice platforms. (AFEJAD, 2023) Nonetheless, most of these remain informational (e.g., market prices, weather, advisory messages) rather than realtime sensing or automated decision-support systems.Therefore, although Africa is making progress in digitalizing agriculture, there remains a clear gap, very limited deployment of integrated IoT + AI-based smart farming systems targeted at smallholder farms, and insufficient evidence on scalable, affordable, and inclusive models that can work under rural Africa’s socio-economic and infrastructural constraints.In East Africa, maize remains a staple crop for many smallholder farmers. The region often faces multiple agronomic challenges such as erratic rainfall, drought, soil fertility degradation, and pest outbreaks  most notably the Fall Armyworm (FAW), which has severely threatened maize production across countries in recent years. To address such challenges, there have been pilot efforts and proposals for deploying digital agriculture solutions but most remain limited to information delivery (advisories, warnings) using SMS, radio, or extension agent visits. There are only a handful of documented cases where sensor-based monitoring or IoT systems have been applied in smallholder maize farming in East Africa. For instance, a 2025 framework proposed for Uganda outlines using IoT to support climate-resilient maize production, highlighting potential for resource-efficient irrigation, yield improvement, and sustainable farming in lowresource contexts. (Nomugisha & Mwebaze, 2025)However, these remain primarily conceptual or small-scale frameworks; widespread, fieldtested IoT + AI systems remain rare. Factors limiting adoption include limited connectivity in rural areas, lack of infrastructure (electricity, network), cost of sensors/maintenance, and limited digital literacy or capacity for farmers. As a result, the region lacks scalable models that integrate realtime soil and environmental sensing, AI-driven decision support, automated irrigation/pest management, and inclusive communication channels (e.g., for farmers without smartphones).Rwanda’s economy still relies heavily on agriculture, with many smallholder farmers cultivating staple crops such as maize. The government and stakeholders have recognized the potential of digital agriculture: according to the Rwanda Agriculture and Animal Resources Development Board (RAB) and international partners, digital agriculture could help modernize farming, improve productivity, and enhance food security in the face of climate variability and limited land resources. (African Development Bank, FAO, & CGIAR, 2021) Recent national-level innovation shows movement: as of 2024, RAB launched the Rwanda Digital Soil Information System (RwaSIS), designed to provide farmers with detailed, locationspecific soil composition data and tailored fertilizer recommendations, for crops including maize, rice, beans, and cassava. (RAB, 2024)  This is a major step toward precision agriculture and demonstrates national commitment to data-driven farming.This development shows that Rwanda is gradually adopting digital agriculture tools beyond simple advisory services. However, even this promising initiative focuses on soil fertility and fertilizer optimization, It doesn’t yet integrate real-time environmental monitoring (e.g., soil moisture, humidity), irrigation scheduling, pest detection or automated actions, nor does it provide inclusive real-time alerts to farmers via accessible channels (e.g., USSD) in rural settings. Thus, there remains a clear gap between current digital agriculture efforts in Rwanda and the full potential of “smart farming. Specifically There is no widely used sensor-based IoT system for smallholder maize farms that provides continuous monitoring of soil moisture, temperature, humidity, or pest risk. There is no integrated AIdriven decision support for irrigation optimization or pest management (e.g., early detection of FAW) connected to farmer communication platforms. Existing digital tools primarily address input/fertilizer recommendations or advisory services, but lack real-time feedback, automation, and inclusive communication (especially for farmers without smartphones).Given the global rise of Agriculture 4.0 and promising advances in IoT and data-driven farming, and considering Africa’s ongoing digitalagriculture uptake yet limited to basic ICT interventions it is timely to explore and develop inclusive, affordable, IoTbased smart farming systems tailored to smallholder contexts.In Rwanda, with initiatives such as RwaSIS already underway, there is institutional openness to data-driven agriculture. But to truly modernize maize farming one of the country’s staple and vital crops there is a need for systems that go beyond soil-fertility mapping. Systems that monitor in real time soil water status, environmental parameters, and pest risk, that support automated or semiautomated responses (e.g., irrigation, pesticide alerts) and that deliver information and actionable advice to farmers via accessible channels (e.g., GSM/SMS/USSD), including those without smartphones.This research “IoT-Based Smart Maize Farming with AI-Driven Decision Support for Irrigation Optimization and Fall Armyworm Control” aims to fill precisely this gap. It seeks to design, prototype, and evaluate a system that integrates sensors, AI analytics, decision-support logic, and inclusive communication, capable of improving resource use, crop yield, pest resilience, and supporting smallholder farmers in real conditions. The AI model combines real-time sensor readings, weather forecasts, and image-based FAW detection to trigger preventive or corrective irrigation and pest management responses. In doing so, the study contributes to bridging global smart-farming advances with the realities and needs of smallholder agriculture in Rwanda, offering a potentially scalable, replicable model for similar contexts elsewhere in Africa.1.3 Statement of the ProblemMaize farmers in Rwamagana District continue to face persistent challenges that significantly undermine productivity, including erratic rainfall patterns, inefficient irrigation practices, declining soil fertility, and the increasing prevalence of pests such as the Fall Armyworm (FAW) (Spodoptera frugiperda) (FAO, 2021; RAB, 2022). These challenges contribute to substantial yield losses, inefficient use of water and fertilizers, and heightened economic vulnerability among smallholder farmers, who typically cultivate small landholdings and rely on traditional or experience-based farming practices.Although Rwanda has implemented several digital agriculture initiatives, including soil information platforms, mobile-based advisory services, and fertilizer recommendation systems, these solutions primarily deliver static or generalized recommendations. They lack continuous, field-level data acquisition, real-time pest surveillance, and automated decision support tailored to individual farms. Furthermore, most existing systems assume access to smartphones and internet connectivity, limiting their effectiveness among smallholder farmers who depend on basic mobile phones (African Development Bank, 2022; Business Insider, 2025).To address these limitations, this research introduces a novel, integrated IoT-based smart maize farming system that combines real-time environmental sensing, AI-driven decision support, and inclusive GSM/USSD communication. Unlike existing approaches, the proposed system fuses sensor-generated field data with AI-based pest detection using farmer-submitted crop images to enable early FAW identification and context-specific recommendations. The system further incorporates automated and data-driven irrigation scheduling and nutrient management, moving beyond advisory services toward actionable, precision-based farm management. Importantly, by leveraging GSM/USSD technology, the system ensures accessibility for farmers without smartphones, thereby bridging the digital divide in rural agricultural settings.By introducing a unified, real-time, and smallholder-inclusive decision-support framework, the proposed system aims to improve resource-use efficiency, enhance resilience to climatic and pest-related risks, and increase maize productivity in Rwamagana District. This research therefore addresses a critical gap in Rwanda’s agricultural digitization efforts by transitioning from generalized advisory platforms to an intelligent, adaptive, and farm-specific smart farming solution that supports sustainable smallholder maize production.1.4 Research Objectives1.4.1. IntroductionThis chapter presents a review of existing scholarly work related to IoT-based smart agriculture, AI-driven decision support systems, irrigation optimization, and FAW detection and management. It begins by examining key theoretical and conceptual foundations that guide the study, followed by an analysis of empirical studies conducted globally, regionally, and locally. The chapter also explores the technologies, models, and frameworks previously applied in precision agriculture, identifies gaps in past research, and positions the current study within those gaps. Overall, the literature review provides the foundation upon which the study’s methodology and contributions are developed.2.2 Key Concepts2.2.1. Internet of Things (IoT)IoT refers to interconnected physical devices embedded with sensors, software, and connectivity that enable them to collect and exchange data autonomously (Ashton, 2020). In agriculture, IoT facilitates real-time monitoring of soil, weather, and crop conditions.2.2.2. Smart FarmingSmart farming describes the application of digital technologies such as IoT, automation, drones, and AIto optimize agricultural processes, reduce resource wastage, and improve productivity (FAO, 2021).2.2.3. Artificial Intelligence (AI)AI involves computational systems capable of performing tasks that normally require human intelligence, including decision-making, pattern recognition, and prediction. In agriculture, AI supports disease detection, yield prediction, and precision irrigation (Li et al., 2020).2.2.4. Decision Support System (DSS)A DSS is an interactive information system that uses data analytics and models to help users make informed and timely decisions (Hesabi et al., 2023). In this study, DSS refers to the AI-powered system providing irrigation and pest-management recommendations.2.2.5. Irrigation OptimizationThis involves the use of data-driven techniques to determine the most efficient timing, quantity, and frequency of irrigation to minimize water loss and maximize crop productivity (World Bank, 2022).2.2.6. Fall Armyworm (FAW)FAW (Spodoptera frugiperda) is a highly destructive crop pest affecting maize production in Africa. It spreads rapidly, feeding on maize leaves and reducing yields significantly if not controlled early (African Development Bank, 2022; RAB Report, 2022).2.2.7. PestigationPestigation is an integrated farming practice where pesticides are applied through irrigation systems, enabling simultaneous water and pest-control delivery with improved efficiency (Business Insider, 2025).2.3 Conceptual ReviewThis study is anchored on a conceptual model that integrates IoT sensing, AI-driven analytics, and farmer interaction to optimize maize production. The conceptual framework highlights how digital technologies can enhance early pest detection, precision irrigation, and farmer decision-making. Recent studies highlight the growing use of multi-source data fusion in smart farming systems, where IoT sensor readings are combined with weather forecasts and image-based pest detection to improve prediction accuracy and automate decision-making. Integrating environmental data with AI-driven visual diagnostics enables early detection of threats such as Fall Armyworm and supports preventive or corrective irrigation and pest management strategies (Li et al., 2020; Hesabi et al., 2023).2.3.1 IoT Sensors in Precision AgricultureIoT technologies allow real-time sensing of soil moisture, temperature, humidity, and crop health. Previous studies demonstrate that soil-moisture sensors significantly reduce water wastage while maintaining or increasing yields (FAO, 2021). In Rwanda, the adoption of IoT in smallholder farming remains limited, yet evidence suggests high potential for improving climate-sensitive crops like maize (World Bank, 2022).2.3.2 AI-Driven Decision Support SystemsAI models have proven effective in classifying plant diseases, predicting irrigation needs, and supporting resource management. Hesabi et al. (2023) show that machine-learning-based DSS can improve accuracy in farm decision-making by up to 40%. For FAW detection, deep-learning image-recognition models offer promising results when trained on local pest images (Li et al., 2020).2.3.3 Fall Armyworm Early Detection and ResponseFAW continues to threaten maize production in East Africa, causing severe yield losses among smallholder farmers (African Development Bank, 2022). Early detection is essential, yet farmers often rely on manual scouting, which is slow and inconsistent (RAB, 2022). Integrating mobile cameras or field-based imaging devices enables rapid reporting, classification, and recommendation generation.2.3.4 Integrated Pestigation and Irrigation SchedulingSmart irrigation systems that combine irrigation with pest control (pestigation) have been recognized as efficient for controlling pests while reducing labor costs (Business Insider, 2025). IoT sensors provide real-time data to automate water flow decisions, while AI recommendations ensure precise pesticide application.2.3.5 Fertigation in Smart FarmingFertigation is the application of watersoluble fertilizers through irrigation systems, delivering nutrients directly to the crop root zone in a controlled and timely manner (Kim, Lee, Oh, & Sung, 2022). This approach improves nutrientuse and wateruse efficiency, reduces fertilizer losses, and enhances crop yield compared to conventional fertilization methods (Ashrafi, Raj, Shamim, Lal, & Kumar, 2020). When integrated with IoT and AI-driven systems, fertigation can be dynamically adjusted based on real-time soil and environmental data, making it a key component of precision and sustainable agriculture (Hari Krishna et al., 2020).2.3.6 Farmer Engagement Through Mobile PlatformsMany digital agriculture systems succeed only when farmers can interact with them easily. Mobile-based platforms allow farmers to upload pest images, receive instant feedback, and follow recommended irrigation schedules. Research in Sub-Saharan Africa shows that mobile-based advisory systems significantly improve adoption of modern farming technologies (FAO, 2021). 2.4 Theoretical ReviewThis study is grounded in the following key theories that guide the technological and behavioral dimensions of smart agriculture systems:2.4.1 Precision Agriculture TheoryPrecision Agriculture Theory emphasizes using technology to apply the right input, at the right place, at the right time, and in the right quantity. IoT sensors, data analytics, and automated systems align directly with this principle. The theory supports the study's objective of optimizing irrigation and pesticide application through data-driven insights (FAO, 2021).2.4.2 Diffusion of Innovation Theory (Rogers, 2003)This theory explains how new technologies spread within a community. It is relevant because the adoption of IoT-based smart farming in Rwanda depends on farmers’ perceptions of usefulness, ease of use, and compatibility with existing practices. The theory helps analyze factors influencing the uptake of AI-enabled systems among smallholder farmers in Rwamagana.2.4.3 Decision Support System TheoryDSS Theory suggests that effective decisions result from the integration of data, models, and user-friendly interfaces. This aligns with the study’s AI-driven decision-support component, which transforms sensor and image data into actionable recommendations for irrigation and FAW control (Hesabi et al., 2023).2.4.4 Pest Management TheoryThis theory emphasizes integrated pest management approaches (IPM), which combine monitoring, prevention, biological control, and targeted chemical interventions. The AI-enabled detection component and pestigation insights in this study correspond to IPM principles by supporting early detection and precise pesticide use (Li et al., 2020).2.4.5.Technology Acceptance Model (TAM)The Technology Acceptance Model (TAM), proposed by Davis (1989), provides a theoretical framework for understanding users’ acceptance and adoption of new technologies. TAM posits that two key factors Perceived Usefulness (PU) and Perceived Ease of Use (PEOU) determine an individual’s behavioral intention to use a technology, which subsequently predicts actual usage. . In agricultural contexts, farmers are more likely to adopt IoT solutions if they believe the technology will improve their productivity, reduce risks, and save resources, and if the system is easy to understand and operate (Kariuki et al., 2020). 2.4.6 Summary of Theoretical ReviewThe theoretical review identifies several frameworks relevant to the adoption and implementation of IoT-based smart farming systems. Among these, the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) and Diffusion of Innovation (DOI) theory serve as the primary foundations. TAM explains user adoption of new technologies based on perceived usefulness and perceived ease of use, which directly informs the design and user interaction of the proposed IoT + AI system. DOI provides insight into how innovations spread within a community, highlighting factors that influence adoption among smallholder farmers.In addition, supporting theories such as general ICT adoption frameworks, precision agriculture adoption models, and sensor technology acceptance models were reviewed to contextualize technology uptake and diffusion in agricultural settings. While these supporting theories help explain broader adoption challenges and opportunities, TAM and DOI remain central to this study, providing the key lens for understanding how smallholder farmers in Rwanda might adopt, use, and benefit from the proposed system.2.5 Empirical StudyEmpirical research on the integration of IoT and AI in smart farming has significantly grown over recent years, reflecting the rapid adoption of digital technologies for precision agriculture. These studies collectively indicate that the deployment of IoT sensors and AI systems can enhance resource efficiency, improve crop monitoring, and support predictive decisionmaking compared with traditional farming methods (Miller et al., 2025). Several fieldoriented research efforts have empirically evaluated the benefits of IoTenabled monitoring combined with AI analytics. Early researches documented that IoT sensors measuring soil moisture, temperature, and environmental conditions, paired with AI decision support, increased wheat yield by approximately 23% and reduced water consumption by 40% over a full cropping season compared with conventional methods. This study also reported cost savings and improved crop health management, showing the tangible impact of smart farming technologies on both productivity and sustainability in real agricultural environments (Akbar et al., 2025). In similar research, Singh et al. (2025) evaluated precision farming applications that integrate realtime IoT data with AI models for irrigation and disease detection. Their empirical findings revealed water usage reductions of 20–50% and disease detection accuracies exceeding 90%, demonstrating that such systems not only optimize limited resources but also enable timely responses to crop health issues. These results underscore the capacity of integrated AIIoT frameworks to transform traditional practices into datadriven management strategies under diverse farming conditions (Singh et al., 2025). Despite these positive outcomes, empirical research also identifies persistent challenges. Many studies report high infrastructure costs, connectivity limitations in rural areas, and barriers related to interoperability among devices as key constraints on broader adoption, particularly in resourcelimited contexts. The digital divide observed in the literature suggests that while IoT and AI solutions deliver measurable efficacy in datarich environments, localized adaptation and supportive policies are necessary to ensure sustainable and equitable deployment in smallholder farming systems (Miller et al., 2025). Overall, empirical evidence indicates that IoT and AI integration in agriculture can produce quantifiable gains in efficiency, yield, and sustainability when compared with traditional practices. These findings provide an essential empirical foundation that justifies ongoing research and implementation efforts, particularly within developing agricultural economies seeking to leverage precision technologies for food security and environmental stewardship.2.6.
